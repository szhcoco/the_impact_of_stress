The Science Behind Stress

Understanding how physiological responses impact cognitive performance

The Yerkes-Dodson Law

This century-old principle reveals that performance increases with physiological or mental arousal—but only up to a point. Beyond this optimal zone, performance deteriorates.

Our data visualizes this relationship in an academic setting, showing how different stress levels correlate with exam performance.

Physiological Indicators

During exams, students experience measurable physiological changes:

  • Electrodermal Activity (EDA) - Measures skin conductance affected by sweat
  • Heart Rate Variability (HRV) - Indicates autonomic nervous system activity
  • Blood Volume Pulse (BVP) - Measures blood flow changes
  • Temperature - Fluctuations in peripheral body temperature

These signals combine to create our Composite Stress Index (CSI).

Physiological Signals

Electrodermal activity (EDA) shows the most significant response during stressful moments, correlating with performance dips.

Optimal Stress Zone

Student 1 performed best at a stress index of 0.62 during Midterm 1, achieving 84% on that exam.

This reveals the Yerkes-Dodson Law in action - moderate stress enhances cognitive performance.

Recovery Patterns

After difficult questions, Student 1 showed a 42% faster recovery during Midterm 2 compared to Midterm 1.

This increased resilience correlated with a 12% grade improvement despite similar content difficulty.

Critical Moments

At minute 48 of the Final Exam, Student 1 experienced a temperature drop of 0.8°C coinciding with a challenging problem.

This physiological response preceded a 15% performance dip in subsequent questions.

Key Takeaways

What we've learned about stress and performance

Stress Isn't the Enemy

Our data clearly shows that moderate stress levels correlate with peak performance. The challenge is not eliminating stress, but managing it effectively.

Recovery Matters

Students who recovered quickly from stressful moments performed better overall, regardless of how many stressful events they encountered.

Predictive Patterns

Physiological responses like temperature drops often preceded performance declines by several minutes, suggesting opportunities for intervention.
