Decoding the Physiology of Exam Stress

A comprehensive analysis of how physiological responses correlate with academic performance across exam periods

"Stress is not what happens to us. It's our physiological response to what happens. And in an academic setting, these responses reveal fascinating patterns that predict performance."

The Metaphysics - Why Are We Here?

Intriguing Background: It has long been established that stress can significantly impact academic performance, yet the physiological mechanisms behind this relationship remain poorly understood. While Heart Rate and EDA (Electrodermal Activity) are commonly studied and acknowledged as key indicator of stress level, their interplay with cognitive load and emotional state may not be as clear as we typically think of.
Motivation: In order to investigate the capability to regard heart rate and EDA as reliable indicators of academic stress, it is essential to analyze their patterns in relation to cognitive load and emotional state during exam periods. While that largely depends on more data in further follow-up researches, initial findings suggest little correlations between them.

Key Insights from Physiological Data

The Fluctuating Heart

Students typically have fluctuated heart rates, particularly at the middle of the tests.

This also happens in the very beginning and end of the exam.

Patternless EDA

On the other hand, EDA of all students exhibits extraordinary chaos, bringing in the doubt of whether this could be a meaningful bio-signal for stress.

The Warning

From almost all students in almost all exams, there is little pattern showing that they align with each other.

We may have to revisit this long-established view of these bio-signals.
